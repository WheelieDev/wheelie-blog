For years I wore exhaustion like a medal. The late finishes, the skipped lunches, the phone that never went off — I told myself this was what commitment looked like. If I was tired enough, surely it meant I cared enough. The tiredness was the proof.

It took a proper crash to understand how wrong that was. Not a dramatic one — no single moment I could point to. Just a slow grey flattening, where the work I loved started to feel like a weight I couldn't put down, and the people I wanted to help became, on the worst days, a list of demands I resented. That was the part that frightened me. Burnout doesn't only take your energy. It takes your warmth.

## The maths doesn't work

Here is the thing nobody says often enough in caring professions: **you cannot pour from an empty cup, and pretending you can just means everyone gets less.**

> Looking after myself wasn't selfish. It was the only way to keep showing up for the people who needed me.

Running on empty doesn't make you a hero. It makes you slower, blunter, and more likely to miss the very things that matter most — the small signals, the change in someone's mood, the detail that turns out to be important. The exhausted version of me was not the dedicated one. He was just the one who hadn't stopped yet.

## What actually helped

I won't pretend I have this solved. But some things genuinely moved the needle:

- **Treating rest as part of the job, not a reward for it.** The break is not what you get *after* the work. It is what makes the work possible.
- **Letting people in.** Saying "I'm struggling" out loud, to a colleague or a manager, broke the spell more than any amount of pushing through ever did.
- **Guarding the edges of the day.** A real start, a real finish, and a phone that is allowed to be off.

If you do this work — caring for others, in whatever form that takes — please hear this from someone who learned it late: your wellbeing is not a luxury you'll get to eventually. It is the foundation everything else stands on. Look after the carer, and the caring takes care of itself.
