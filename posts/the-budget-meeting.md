There is a meeting that happens in every council, every year, that nobody volunteers to chair. It has an agenda, a set of papers thicker than a phone book, and a coffee flask that runs out far too early. And it carries a weight that no agenda item can quite describe, because every number on the spreadsheet in front of you is a service, and every service is a person.

You learn quickly that "efficiency savings" is the politest phrase in the English language. It can mean a great many things, and almost none of them are painless. Behind a two per cent line reduction is a day centre that opens four days a week instead of five. Behind a rounding error is someone's afternoon, their company, their reason to leave the house.

## Everything is connected to everything

The hardest part is not the arithmetic. The hardest part is that the system is a web, not a list. Cut the early help and you pay for the crisis later. Trim the bus subsidy and the GP appointments don't get attended. Save money on prevention and spend it threefold on acute care eighteen months down the line.

> Every number on the spreadsheet is a service, and every service is a person.

A good budget meeting is really an argument about time horizons. The pressure is always to balance *this* year, because that is what the law requires and what the headlines measure. The responsibility is to protect the years after that, which nobody will thank you for and which rarely make the front page.

## Keeping the person in the room

So we try, imperfectly, to keep the person in the room — even when they aren't there. Some of the ways that help:

1. **Name the human cost out loud.** Not "a reduction in domiciliary hours" but "fewer morning visits for people who live alone."
2. **Ask who is missing from the table.** The people most affected by these decisions are almost never in the meeting.
3. **Protect prevention first.** It is the easiest thing to cut and the most expensive thing to lose.

I won't pretend we always get the balance right. Budgets are blunt instruments and the constraints are real. But the meeting nobody wants to chair is, in the end, one of the most honest expressions of what a council is *for* — a community deciding, together and out loud, what it refuses to let fall.
