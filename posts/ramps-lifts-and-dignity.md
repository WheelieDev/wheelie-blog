I have been carried up a flight of steps by two well-meaning strangers. I have been parked next to the bins because that was where the "accessible" entrance happened to be. I have been ushered through a restaurant kitchen, past the chefs and the deliveries, because it was — and I quote — "just easier this way." Each time, the people involved were kind. Each time, I left feeling like a problem that had been solved rather than a guest who had been welcomed.

This is the thing about access that the regulations never quite capture. A building can be technically compliant and still tell you, in a hundred small ways, that you were not the person it was built for.

## Good access is invisible

The best accessible design is the kind you never notice. A gentle ramp that everyone uses without thinking. A lift by the main entrance, not down a corridor and through a fire door. A level threshold, a wide aisle, a counter at a height that lets you look someone in the eye instead of up at them.

> Good access is quiet. Bad access is a daily reminder of who a place was really built for.

When it works, nobody talks about it, because there is nothing to talk about. You arrive the same way everyone else arrives. You are simply *there*, like anyone else. That ordinariness is the whole point — and it is astonishing how often it is treated as an optional extra.

## The afterthought tax

The trouble with bolting access on at the end is that it always shows. The ramp at the side. The buzzer you have to press and wait at. The accessible toilet doubling as a store cupboard. These are the small humiliations of being designed for last, and they add up to a quiet message: *you were not part of the plan.*

It doesn't have to be this way. Designing for access from the first sketch rather than the final sign-off costs little and changes everything. A few principles I'd put on every architect's wall:

- **Same door, same welcome.** If disabled people use a different entrance, you've already failed.
- **Design for the edge and you improve the middle.** Step-free access helps parents with buggies, delivery drivers, and tired feet alike.
- **Ask, then build.** The people who use a space know things no specification ever will.

Getting in should not be a feat. It should not require gratitude, or two strong strangers, or a detour past the bins. It should just be the door — open, level, and meant for you.
