There is a particular quality to the light at seven in the morning, and a particular weight to the day that hasn't started yet. This is the hour where "person-centred care" stops being a phrase on a laminated poster and becomes a series of very small, very human decisions. Which mug. Which radio station. Whether today is a good day for a walk, or a day for staying close to home.

We have, I think, talked the phrase nearly to death. It appears in policy documents and inspection frameworks and the opening slide of more training sessions than I can count. And the more we say it, the more it risks meaning nothing at all — a box to tick rather than a way of being with another person.

## The gap between the plan and the doorstep

A care plan is a useful thing. It records what matters, it keeps everyone honest, and it means the person isn't starting from scratch with every new face that comes through the door. But a plan is a map, not the territory. The territory is a kitchen at dawn, a person who slept badly, and a hundred choices that no document could ever anticipate.

Person-centred care lives in that gap. It is the willingness to put the plan down for a moment and ask: *what does this person, today, actually want?*

> The smallest choices are where dignity either survives or quietly disappears.

I've watched brilliant carers do this instinctively. They notice that someone always reaches for the blue cup. They learn that the news makes one person anxious and another feel connected to the world. They understand that "getting someone up and dressed" and "helping someone start their day well" are not the same task, even when they look identical from the outside.

## What it asks of us

Doing this properly is not soft, and it is not easy. It asks for:

- **Time** — the one resource the system is most reluctant to give.
- **Attention** — noticing the small signals that no assessment captures.
- **Humility** — accepting that the person, not the professional, is the expert on their own life.

None of that fits neatly on a spreadsheet. But it is the whole job. Everything else — the funding, the rotas, the paperwork — exists to make those seven o'clock moments possible.

So when I hear "person-centred", I try not to picture the policy. I picture the kettle, the blue cup, and the quiet, ordinary act of letting someone choose how their own day begins.
